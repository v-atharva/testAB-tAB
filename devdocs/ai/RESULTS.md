# What we found, in plain language

We re-analyzed every A/B test Upworthy ran between 2013 and 2015 — 32,487
experiments on headlines and images — using the statistics a careful
experimentation team would apply today. Three things stand out.

**Winning results were roughly double reality.** When a variant "wins" a test,
it usually wins partly because it's genuinely better and partly because it got
lucky — and the luck doesn't repeat. The median winning headline claimed a 96%
click-rate improvement; our corrected estimate is 50%. Anyone planning around
the raw numbers would have expected about 1.7× more improvement than they
could actually get. The smaller the test, the worse the inflation.

**Most tests couldn't answer their own question.** Upworthy's true headline
effects were big by industry standards (a typical real lift was ~35%), yet the
median test had only a 32% chance of detecting an effect that size, because
tests were stopped after a few thousand viewers per arm. Fifty-six percent of
all tests end in our analysis as "underpowered — don't conclude," and only 1 in
29 met the standard 80% power bar. More traffic per test — meaning fewer,
bigger tests — would have bought much more knowledge for the same audience.

**Standard hygiene checks change the story.** After correcting for testing
many variants at once and for hunting across thousands of experiments, 69% of
apparent "wins" evaporate. A seventh of all tests show traffic imbalances that
make them untrustworthy. And of the winners Upworthy's own tooling declared,
only about one in five holds up.

None of this required new data — only honest statistics. The dashboard in this
project shows what every one of those 32,487 readouts should have looked like.
